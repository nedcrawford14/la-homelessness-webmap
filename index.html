Conclusion

Los Angeles County’s homelessness crisis is not randomly distributed; it follows clear spatial patterns shaped by economic pressure, housing instability, and lived conditions on the ground.

The relationship between high rent burden, elevated homelessness rates, and concentrated 311 encampment calls illustrates how systemic affordability challenges translate into visible and measurable impacts across the region.

While each dataset tells part of the story, together they reveal a consistent geography of vulnerability that demands coordinated, data-driven responses.

Addressing homelessness in Los Angeles will require not only immediate intervention where need is most concentrated, but also long-term strategies that reduce housing cost burdens and prevent displacement before it occurs.

Methodology

This project analyzes homelessness in Los Angeles County through a spatial framework that connects housing affordability, population-normalized homelessness rates, and public reporting patterns.

Homelessness data were normalized by population at the census tract level to allow for more meaningful comparisons across areas of varying density. Housing cost burden was calculated using American Community Survey data, while 311 service requests were used to generate a kernel density surface representing the spatial concentration of encampment-related calls.

Data processing and spatial analysis were conducted in ArcGIS Pro. Final cartographic design, web publishing, and narrative presentation were completed through ArcGIS Online and ArcGIS StoryMaps.
